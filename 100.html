100-200

00
— Lions became extinct in the Balkans, bricks become the primary building material in the Roman Empire and paper is used by the general populace in China.
10
— An earthquake destroys Apamea and Antioch in Syria. The local bishop is held responsible. Pope Sixtus I succeeds Alexander I as the seventh pope of Rome.
20
— Plague sweeps North Africa in the wake of a locust invasion that destroys large areas of cropland. The plague kills as many as 500,000 in Numidia and possibly 150,000 on the coast before moving to Italy, where it takes so many lives that villages and towns are abandoned.
30
— A law is passed in Rome banning the execution of slaves without a trial. Chinese scientist Zhang Heng invents the first seismometer for determining the exact cardinal direction of earthquakes hundreds of miles away.
40
— The export of olive oil from Hispania Baetica to Rome peaks. Festivals to celebrate the 900th anniversary of the founding of Rome begin.
50
— The Germans of the east move south, into the Carpathians and Black Sea area. Antoninus Liberalis writes a work on mythology.
60
— In Rome, the manufacturing of soap containing grease, lime and ashes begins. The first Buddhist monks arrive in China. Arrian, Greek historian and writer, publishes Indica, a work on India and its people.
70
— The churches in southern Gaul are destroyed after a crowd accuses the local Christians of practicing cannibalism. Meditations by Marcus Aurelius is written, in Greek, while on military campaigns in Pannonia.
80
— Galen's popular work on hygiene is published. Cleomedes discovers the refraction of light by the Earth's atmosphere. A supernova now known as SN 185 is noted by Chinese astronomers in the Astrological Annals of the Houhanshu, making it the earliest recorded supernova.
90
— Cleomedes teaches that the moon does not glow on its own, but rather reflects sunlight. Galen writes his manual on pathology, The Art of Curing.
