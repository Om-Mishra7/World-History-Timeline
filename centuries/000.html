00
— Literary works from the decade include works from the ancient Roman poet Ovid; the Ars Amatoria, an instructional elegy series in three books, Metamorphoses, a poem which chronicles the history of the world from its creation to the deification of Julius Caesar within a loose mythico-historical framework, and Ibis, a curse poem written during his years in exile across the Black Sea for an offense against Augustus. Nicolaus of Damascus wrote the 15-volume History of the World.
10
— In the Roman Empire, an edict was issued effecting an empire-wide ban on divinatory practices especially astrology. The edict requires any consultation between a customer and a practitioner to be conducted with at least one third party witness present and bans inquiry into anyone's death.
20
— In science, the 20s saw the manufacture of pens and metal writing tools in Rome. Major disasters of this decade include a fire in Rome, and the collapse of a poorly built amphitheatre in Fidenae, which killed 20,000 of the 50,000 spectators. In 27, Christianity was born as a Jewish sect in Jerusalem. Geographica, an encyclopedia of geographical knowledge created by Strabo, was finished no later than year 23.
30
— Jesus was crucified early in the decade: his suffering and redemptive death would form central aspects of Christian theology concerning the doctrines of salvation and atonement. Peter the Apostle founded the Church of Antioch. Anti-Jewish riots broke out in Alexandria. A financial crisis hit Rome in year 33.
40
— Christianity came to Egypt as the Church of Alexandria was founded with Mark the Evangelist as the first Patriarch. James the Great died in year 44: One of the Twelve Apostles of Jesus, he was the first to be martyred according to the New Testament. Claudius expelled the Jews from Rome.
50
— Londinium, which will later become the city of London, is founded by the Romans. They also start building a bridge over the Thames, in the same area. In today's Afghanistan and northern India, the Yuezhi tribes are united under the Kushan leader Kujula Kadphises, thus creating the Kushan Empire. In Southern America, the San Bartolo pyramid is completed around this time. The Yuejue Shu, the first known gazetteer of China, is written during these years. Vonones II dies after a few months on the throne. His son Vologases I takes his place as king of the Parthian Empire and soon declares war on the Roman Empire.
60
— The Roxolani were defeated on the Danube by the Romans. Gaius Suetonius Paulinus, Roman governor of Britain, captured the island of Mona (Anglesey), the last stronghold of the Druids.Iceni and Trinovantes first destroyed the Roman capital Camulodunum (Colchester), wiped out the infantry of the Legio IX Hispana and went on to burn Londinium (London) and Verulamium (St Albans), in all cases massacring the inhabitants in thousands.
70
— India saw the end of the Hellenistic dynasties.Panic striked Rome as adverse winds delayed grain shipments from Africa and Egypt, producing a bread shortage. Ships laden with wheat from North Africa sailed 300 miles to Rome's port of Ostia in 3 days. The vessels often carried 1,000 tons each to provide the city with the 8,000 tons per week it normally consumed.
80
— Emperor Titus completed and inaugurated the Colosseum with 100 days of games. The aeolipile, the first steam engine, was described by Hero of Alexandria. The Gospel of Luke and Acts of the Apostles were written. Some 30,000 Asian tribesmen migrated from the steppes to the west with 40,000 horses and 100,000 cattle, joining with Iranian tribesmen and with Mongols from the Siberian forests to form a group that will be known in Europe as the Huns.
90
— The Young Flavian Woman was made. It is now kept at Musei Capitolini, Rome.The Gospel of John was drafted. The First Dacian War ended and Decebalus became the client king of Rome, he received money, craftsmen and war machines to protect the borders of the Roman Empire.
